The Seven Wonders of the Ancient World

Brief facts and locations

  1. Great Pyramid of Giza (Egypt)
    Built c. 2580–2560 BCE as a tomb for Pharaoh Khufu. The only one of the seven still largely intact; remarkable for its original height (~146.6 m).
  2. Hanging Gardens of Babylon (Iraq — disputed)
    Legendary terraced gardens attributed to Nebuchadnezzar II (6th century BCE). Descriptions suggest an engineered oasis of terraces, waterworks, and exotic plants.
  3. Statue of Zeus at Olympia (Greece)
    Massive seated chryselephantine (gold and ivory) statue by Phidias, c. 435 BCE, located in the Temple of Zeus; celebrated for its scale and artistry.
  4. Temple of Artemis at Ephesus (Turkey)
    A grand Ionic temple rebuilt multiple times; famous for its size, ornate sculptures, and role as a major religious center (c. 6th–4th centuries BCE).
  5. Mausoleum at Halicarnassus (Bodrum, Turkey)
    Tomb of Mausolus (c. 350 BCE); admired for its lofty stepped design, colonnades, and sculptural decoration. Gave rise to the word "mausoleum."
  6. Colossus of Rhodes (Greece)
    A giant bronze statue of the sun god Helios (c. 280 BCE) standing at Rhodes' harbor; celebrated for its engineering and imposing presence before its destruction.
  7. Lighthouse (Pharos) of Alexandria (Egypt)
    Built on the island of Pharos (3rd century BCE) to guide sailors into Alexandria's harbor; one of the tallest man-made structures of the ancient world.
Notes: "Seven Wonders" here refers to the classical list of ancient constructions.
